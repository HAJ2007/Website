INTRODUCTION

Little bit about me:

I aspire to become a software developer. You might wonder why, well, there’s more than one answer. My number one reason is that software development, or technology itself, feels like a reflection of who I am. I’ve always loved to draw and create. I find a unique satisfaction in building something aesthetically pleasing that requires effort and patience, and I’ve always enjoyed that. But contrarily, I have always loved logic, and have always found it intriguing to solve problems, figure things out, put pieces together to form something new, or take something apart to understand its mysterious inner workings. To me, technology is a culmination of all these interests. While creativity isn’t always obvious in technical roles, I strongly believe that solving real-world problems to some extent requires it. You have to be innovative, think outside the box, and crafty about your solutions that often extend beyond just writing code. The sense of purpose you feel when things work as they should is oddly peaceful.


And that’s why I’m passionate about pursuing software development.


I also have other hobbies that reflect my interests and individuality. I enjoy drawing and painting. Every time I complete a piece, I feel a sense of accomplishment that allows me to channel my creativity. My preferred art style is realism, but I have experimented with other styles. I work best with pencils, but also enjoy using acrylics. This hobby has taught me patience and attention to detail, as well as adaptability, especially when working with watercolours. I enjoy creating, whether it’s a piece of code or an artwork, and I find it thrilling to learn, experiment, grow, and adapt. This reflects my chaotic self that is somehow contained within an orderly self.


I also enjoy reading mostly fictional, non-academic works. Since English is not my first language, I see reading as a way to expand my vocabulary and strengthen my communication. I particularly enjoy books aimed at children and teenagers. They often contain descriptions that uniquely reflect how young people perceive the world, rather than how adults assume they do. When I read them now as a young adult, I find a surprising level of depth, a reminder of how our understanding evolves as we grow, shifting from the explicit to recognising deeper, implicit meanings. I also read scientific articles and academic books, which help me understand more about the subjects I am studying and the ways the world itself is changing.
